Ways to Fight Seasonal Affective Disorder Ahead of the Second Pandemic Winter

By: Nia Freeman | Sept 28, 2021

Woman photo created by dashu83 - www.freepik.com

This time of year, the change into fall and winter, brings many people so much joy. There are pumpkin patches, haunted houses, fall festivals and Christmas carols. The weather will get cooler everywhere, but the amount of time the sun will be in the sky will vary depending different parts of the world. With daylight savings ending in less than two months, this change is sun time and increased darkness, can increase symptoms depression. Season Affective Disorder (SAD), as defined by the National Institute of Mental Health, is a type of depression related to the seasons changing and it begins and ends around the same time every year.

Season affective disorder can affect people in the change from summer to fall and winter but also in the change from winter to spring and summer. Some of the symptoms of SAD are a loss in energy, having a harder time concentrating, and feeling sluggish and irritable. In the change from summer to fall and winter, your symptoms can include oversleeping and a change in appetite.

Here are 5 things you can do now to get ahead of the winter blues

1. Vitamin D- Vitamin D is used for may different things including promoting healthy bones. But with a decrease in sunlight, now maybe the time to increase your vitamin D tablet intake. The sun is most people largest intake of vitamin D, and it is how most people receive vitamin naturally. You can also get vitamin D from foods such as salmon, cheese, mushrooms, and milk. The average adult, aged 18 or older, should take a dose of 600 to 800 IU (15-20 micrograms) daily. 5- 10 minutes of sun exposure a day can also works.

Icons made by Freepik from www.flaticon.com

2. Journaling- Journaling can help you stay on top of your mood daily or weekly. Journal out any good or bad emotions and that will allow you to stay on top of them. It is recommended to journal about 5 to 20 minutes a day. Some people feel it’s better to journal at night to get out all the emotions of the day. Grab that journal and tell it why you are grateful for the day.

Icons made by monkik from www.flaticon.com

3. Exercising- Exercising helps with any form of depression by releasing endorphins, also known as the feel-good hormone. Endorphins help release the body of stress and pain. Exercising helps you get up and moving around. Sound good exercises to do when experiencing SAD, according to Allina Health, are gentle stretching, yoga, and tai chi. Light walks also help because they expose you to vitamin D.

Icons made by photo3idea_studio from www.flaticon.com

4. Light Therapy- Light therapy, by definition, is the treatment of medical or psychiatric conditions (such as SAD) by the controlled application of light. During light therapy, you sit in front of an artificial light source, called a light box. The light is used imitate sunlight. It is recommended to sit in front of the light box at least 30 minutes a day. Be sure to wear sunscreen, as sunburn has been listed as a con of light therapy.

Icons made by photo3idea_studio from www.flaticon.com

5. Sticking to a Routine- Routines are fixed programs, and by sticking to routines, you will expose your body to sun at the same times of the day. Routines will also ensure that you are getting all of you daily meals so that you aren’t overeating. They keep you having something to look forward to and can help ease symptoms of SAD.

Now is the perfect time to start implementing these practices to get yourself ready for winter. Summer will be back before you know it!
